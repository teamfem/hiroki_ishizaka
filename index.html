Research Attitude and Perspectives for Applications
I work on the geometric foundations of numerical analysis—primarily the finite element method (FEM)—with emphasis on interpolation error estimates and geometric conditions. My published contributions include anisotropic and nonconforming settings, new geometric parameters and quasi-regularity conditions, and a framework that organises error estimates through geometry. These results (and their updates) are documented on the Research page of this site.
Philosophy. Two principles guide my work: invariance and projection. Invariance means results should depend only on the problem’s geometry (the metric), not on arbitrary coordinates or mesh aspect ratios. Projection means algorithms are principled movements onto constrained manifolds (e.g., divergence-free subspaces), making robustness and accuracy legible as geometric design choices. This lens helps connect analysis, geometry, and computation in a single coherent story.
Current focus (what I have done and continue to build):
  • Sharp error estimates on anisotropic/nonconforming meshes under explicit geometric conditions.
  • Development of new geometric parameters and regularity conditions that clarify when optimal approximation is attainable.
  • Organisation of FEM theory around metric-aware error controls and reliable discretisation principles.
Ongoing and prospective directions (explorations, not completed):
  • Extending the same metric-invariant and projection-based ideas toward meshless and learning-based solvers.
  • Linking FEM geometry with information geometry to unify error, robustness, and adaptive design.
  • Pushing toward stochastic PDEs and large-scale anisotropic simulation.
I value open and continuous dissemination—papers, notes, and ideas—independent of affiliation. The aim is simple: a language in which accurate approximation, stability, and practical design become different faces of the same geometric idea.
